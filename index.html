The road to freedom

Ninety years, one direction

From the first uprising to the last viceroy's departure — the movement's turning points.

1857

The first uprising

Sepoys across northern India rose against the East India Company, beginning what many call the First War of Independence.

1885

Indian National Congress founded

A platform for organised political demands took shape, gradually turning scattered discontent into a national movement.

1905

Partition of Bengal & the Swadeshi call

An attempt to divide Bengal backfired, sparking the Swadeshi movement and a boycott of British-made goods in favour of homespun khadi.

1919

Jallianwala Bagh

Troops opened fire on an unarmed gathering in Amritsar, killing hundreds. The massacre turned widespread public opinion irreversibly against colonial rule.

1920

Non-Cooperation Movement

Gandhi called on Indians to withdraw from British institutions, schools, and titles — the first mass movement built on non-violence.

1930

The Salt March

Gandhi walked 240 miles to the sea at Dandi to make salt by hand, defying a British tax and igniting nationwide civil disobedience.

1942

Quit India Movement

With "Do or Die" as its call, the Congress demanded an immediate end to British rule, triggering the largest crackdown of the freedom struggle.

1947

Independence, at midnight

On 15 August, India woke as a free nation. The tricolour was raised over the Red Fort, and a hundred years of struggle found its answer.

Names worth keeping

Remembering the brave

The movement had thousands of hands. These are a few of the names history asks us not to forget.

1869 – 1948

Mahatma Gandhi

Led the movement through non-violent resistance — the Salt March and Quit India among the campaigns that broke colonial resolve without a single weapon raised.

1907 – 1931

Bhagat Singh

Revolutionary who chose the gallows over silence at twenty-three, turning his trial into a public case against imperial rule.

1828 – 1858

Rani Lakshmibai

The Queen of Jhansi rode into battle against the Company's forces, becoming an enduring symbol of the 1857 uprising's defiance.

1897 – 1945

Subhas Chandra Bose

Raised the Indian National Army abroad, believing the British could be forced out by strength as well as by persuasion.

1906 – 1931

Chandrashekhar Azad

Vowed he would never be taken alive, and kept that vow at Alfred Park, Allahabad — a name synonymous with youthful revolt.

1879 – 1949

Sarojini Naidu

Poet and orator known as the Nightingale of India, and the first woman to preside over the Indian National Congress.

1827 – 1857

Mangal Pandey

A sepoy whose act of defiance at Barrackpore is widely remembered as the spark that lit the 1857 rebellion.

1872 – 1901

Alluri Sitarama Raju

Led tribal communities of the Eastern Ghats in armed resistance during the Rampa Rebellion, defying colonial forest laws.
